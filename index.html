2. Workplace Communications
2.1.0 Barriers to Effective Communication

Mental factors: Panic/extreme fear, shock or trauma response, mental health issues
Physiological factors: Poor audio quality, health issues, fatigue or pain, intoxication,

2.1.1 Hearing vs Listening

Hearing is a passive physiological process.
Listening is an active process that involves understanding and comprehending what is heard, which requires attention and engagement.

2.1.2 Active Listening

This is challenging when nonverbal things such as head nods are not an option, so we must do even more with our voice.

Paraphrase and repeat back
Ask clarifying questions
"okay, uh-huh, etc.." is better than nothing

3. Ethics
3.1.1 Why do people make unethical choices?

Not all unethical choices are intentional. They are often made from "close enough" pattern matching that results in drift from policy.
Dispatchers tend to get decertified with around 12-13 years of experience, seemingly due to bad habits compounding and "close enough" choices.

3.1.2 System 1 vs System 2

System 1 is responsible for the automatic reaction to a que or stimulus. System 2 is used when there is an intentional effort made to analyse the situation before reacting.

3.1.3 How to avoid making unethical choices

Make good habits so that System 1 responses will be ethical ones. This is extremely important as System 1 is responsible for 75-90% of behavior.

5.1.2 Perception & Tips to Aid Understanding
Normal:

Perception: Calm, steady voice. Logical, cooperative, answers clearly.
Tips: Build rapport, stay efficient but courteous to maintain calm, gather info now while the caller is stable.

Stimulation:

Perception: Caller sounds nervous, irritated or worried. They may be reporting something new or unexpected. Might ask questions repeatedly or struggle to stay on topic.
Tips: Acknowledge and validate "That sounds really scary, let's get help started" Keep tone calm and even to slow emotional build up. Shorten sentences.

Escalation:

Perception: Raised voice, faster speech, agitation, frustration. May begin yelling, interrupting, accusing. Struggles to answer question or follow directions
Tips: Stay calm and don't mirror their tone. Give short clear directions. Validate emotions without agreeing with any aggression or misinformation.

Crisis:

Perception: Caller may be screaming, crying, incoherent or non-responsive. They could be in active danger or witnessing violence, trauma, or medical emergency. Logic and comprehension are likely impaired.
Tips: Remain calm, show empathy, tell the caller you are there to help, ask and use their name, follow your protocol for call taking, use active listening, ask the person to take some deep breaths, use grounding techniques if possible, mental health crisis? respond accordingly

De-escalation:

Perception: Caller may suddenly go quiet, sound tired, sobbing, apologetic, or spaced out
Tips: Reassure "Help is on the way, you're doing great", normalize the emotional response "It's okay to feel this way", give them something to do "Take a deep breath with me", "Can you tell me what's happening now?"

Stabilization/Normal Baseline:

Perception: Voice steadies, more coherent. Can follow directions and provide more accurate information. May still be emotional but more regulated
Tips: Remember what calmed them down and use it again if needed/remember what activates (triggers) them so it can be avoided, recap and clarify location/who's involved/what help is coming, stay connected until help arrives (if protocol allows), leave them with assurance "Help is on the way. You did the right thing calling us"

*** Remember that every situation will be different ***

6. Resiliency
6.1.1 Effects associated with trauma from a career in public safety

Behavior changes

Hypervigilance, isolation, avoidance, paranoia, change in speech patterns, change in habits addictive behaviors: alcohol, drugs, gambling, etc.

Physical changes

Rapid heartbeat, sweating, nightmares, crying, shaking, headache, loss of or increased appetite little or excessive sleep, fatigue, nausea, increased muscle tension, increased blood pressure, decreased sex drive, chest pain, rapid breathing

Emotional symptoms

Anger, guilt, fear, denial, irritability, shame, depression, shock, overwhelmed, agitation, low self-esteem, loss of trust, decreased mood

Thinking symptoms

Decreased concentration, easily distracted, difficulty reading, negative thoughts, changes in world view, feeling unsafe, uncertainty, poor problem solving skills, hyper-vigilance, suspiciousness, questioning spirituality, blaming

6.1.2 The importance of being able to recognize these effects

Early intervention prevents long-term harm

Public safety professionals‚Äîsuch as police officers, firefighters, EMTs, and dispatchers‚Äîare regularly exposed to high-stress, life-threatening, and emotionally disturbing situations. Over time, this exposure can lead to cumulative trauma, which may develop into serious mental health issues.

Recognizing early signs‚Äîsuch as sleep problems, irritability, emotional numbness, or changes in behavior‚Äîcan lead to timely support, reducing the risk of long-term psychological damage.

Protect job performance and public safety

Unaddressed trauma can impair judgment, decision-making, communication, and emotional regulation. For professionals responsible for protecting others, this can endanger both the public, themselves, and their colleagues. Being able to identify trauma-related effects ensures personnel stay mentally sharp, emotionally resilient, and operationally effective.

Promote a healthier work culture

Recognizing and openly addressing the impact of trauma helps reduce stigma around mental health in public safety fields, where toughness is often overemphasized. This encourages peer support, open communication, and seeking help without shame. A culture that normalizes mental health care is more sustainable and supportive.

Improve retention and career longevity

6.1.3 Coping with the potential impacts of these effects

External processes: movement and talking

Self-care strategies:

Sleep, gratitude, exercise, meditation/yoga, nutrition, professional help, breathe, support system, grounding, family/friends
